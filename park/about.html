About The Park

Our History

Established in 1946, Nairobi National Park holds the distinction of being Kenya's first national park. Its creation marked a significant milestone in Kenya's conservation efforts, setting a precedent for wildlife protection in the country.

The park was gazetted when Nairobi was a much smaller city, but as the capital has grown, the park has remained an important wildlife sanctuary and a symbol of Kenya's commitment to conservation despite urban development.

Timeline of Key Events

  1. 1946: Official establishment of Nairobi National Park
  2. 1963: Management transferred to Kenyan authorities after independence
  3. 1989: Ivory burning ceremony held in the park to demonstrate Kenya's stance against poaching
  4. 2016: Largest ivory burn in history conducted in the park
  5. 2020: New management plan implemented to enhance conservation efforts

Ecosystem & Wildlife

Despite its relatively small size of 117 square kilometers, Nairobi National Park boasts an incredibly diverse ecosystem that supports a wide variety of wildlife.

Flora

The park features a mix of open grass plains, scattered acacia bushes, and riverine forest along the Mbagathi River. This diverse vegetation supports the park's rich biodiversity.

Mammals

  • Black rhinos (endangered species sanctuary)
  • Lions
  • Leopards
  • Cheetahs
  • Buffaloes
  • Giraffes
  • Zebras
  • Various antelope species

Birds

With over 400 recorded bird species, the park is a birdwatcher's paradise. Species range from ostriches to colorful kingfishers and majestic eagles.

Seasonal Migration

During the dry season, wildlife from the Athi-Kapiti plains migrate into the park in search of water and grazing, creating spectacular wildlife viewing opportunities.

Conservation Efforts

Nairobi National Park faces unique conservation challenges due to its proximity to a major urban center. Despite these challenges, the park remains a crucial sanctuary for wildlife and a model for urban conservation.
